AG1 Attribution Analysis

Your Marketing Dashboard Is Lying To You

Meta claimed $246k in revenue. Klaviyo claimed $120k. Shopify recorded $221k. Someone is wrong — and it's not Shopify.

Right now, every DTC brand running Meta Ads and Klaviyo email is looking at numbers that don't add up — and most of them don't know it. They're making budget decisions, scaling campaigns, and cutting channels based on reported revenue that nobody has verified against the source of truth. This is what that actually looks like.

The Attribution Problem Nobody Talks About

Meta uses a 7-day click attribution window. Klaviyo uses a 5-day window. Here's what that means in practice.

A customer clicks a Meta ad on Monday. Opens a Klaviyo email on Wednesday. Buys on Thursday. Meta counts it. Klaviyo counts it. Shopify records one order. You now have two platforms claiming the same $112 sale — and your reported revenue is $224 before you've sold a thing.

Multiply that across thousands of orders and you get this:

Source Reported Revenue Actual Shopify Revenue Overclaim
Meta Ads $246,224 $131,351 +87.5%
Klaviyo Email $120,046 $90,197 +33.1%

Your paid social looks like it's working. Your email looks like it's underperforming. Neither number is real.

What The Real Numbers Actually Say

Strip out the attribution inflation and the story completely changes.

Finding 01 — Paid Social ROAS

The dashboard shows 1.86. The true ROAS is 0.99. The brand thinks paid social is profitable. It's barely breaking even — and that's before creative costs, agency fees, or any overhead hit the P&L.

Finding 02 — Email Has Infinite ROAS

Email drove $90k in revenue with zero ad spend. It's the highest-ROI channel in the business and almost certainly the most underinvested one. Every dollar shifted from Meta to email list growth compounds instead of evaporates.

Finding 03 — LTV Breaks By Channel

Paid social drives mostly one-time buyers. Email drives higher subscription rates and higher lifetime value. The channel that looks less profitable in the dashboard is building the customer base. The one that looks profitable is renting it.

How I Built This

The data is synthetic — built to match real Shopify, Meta Ads, and Klaviyo export formats exactly. Then I made it dirty on purpose.

  • Generated realistic AG1-style ecommerce data with intentional quality issues baked in — 116 duplicate order IDs, null emails, future-dated transactions — the same problems you find in actual brand data exports before anyone has cleaned them
  • Built a three-layer dbt pipeline on BigQuery: staging models that clean each raw source, an intermediate model that deduplicates orders and standardizes channel labels, and mart models that produce the final attribution analysis with true vs. reported revenue side by side
  • Wrote dbt tests that flagged the 116 duplicate order IDs before they ever touched an aggregation — the exact mechanism that causes revenue overcounting in production pipelines when nobody is looking
